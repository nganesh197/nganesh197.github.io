ActiveTrack

Background

Active Track was a partner project between the UCB/UCSF Ergonomics Program and SwiftMotion. This project focused on improving the typically sedentary lifestyle of office workers using a device. This partnership focused on developing as well as validating the startup's (SwiftMotion) product.

Key Takeaways

  • Validating the device against commercially available devices and MVTA (Motion Video Task Analysis)
  • Built a robot arm device to test the accuracy of the Active Track device over a long period.
  • Wrote Python scripts to analyze robot arm data (upwards of 1,000,000 data points), create .csv files, and upload to Google Sheet using Google API with
  • Aided in developing ActiveTrack into a fully fledged product (21 iterations of cases manufacturing, 10 iterations of batteries, and 3 iterations of motors)
  • Took initiative to get special training to work with Polyjet 3D printers in order to create accurate mockups of the case housing before injection molds were made. Helped to reduce protyping costs by few thousand dollars.

Soldering Fan

Background

After spending more than seven years, I have come to the conclusion that soldering fans do not effectively do their intended job, removing solder fumes from the air. This project was driven by: 1) inefficiencies of current soldering fan 2) poor ventilatin while soldering 3)general disregard for precaution against lead exposure in the maker community.

  • Adjustable nozzle to pinpoint solder fume source (solder iron/ solder joint)
  • Speed control of fan to ensure that small parts are not sucked by the device
  • Have a small footprint/ not a hindrance while soldering
  • Use the outlet power
  • Remove 99% of solder fume contaminants from the air

Iteration 1 is currently being tested!

IEOR 170 Project

Background

Project team was tasked with finding a solution that would benefit the healthcare industry through human-centric design. Focusing on ultrasound technicians and their high rate of musculoskeletal injury , especially in the wrist, our solution guides the technician's hand into a comfortable grip that minimizes the risk of overstrenuating the arm muscles while giving the technician enough leverage to provide the force needed to obtain an ultrasound image.

Key Takeaways

  • Designed the detachable ultrasound scanner cuff
  • Aided in gathering responses from ultrasound technicians and developing report of findings
  • Presenting design and prototype with team at Jacobs Hall Design Showcase
